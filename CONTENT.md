## II · THE OPEN LETTER (Substack / Medium / Mirror)

### Letter from the File · Vol. I
### CASE 599 · 8 May 2026

The post is eight words long.

It was written on the morning of June 11, 2022, at 8:30 AM Coordinated Universal Time, by an anonymous user of an English-language platform that, at the time, was still called Twitter. The account that posted it had been opened the day before. It had a profile picture of a hooded figure, a bio that read "reads the future," and three small symbols decorating its profile: an eight-pointed star, an upside-down peace sign, and the Eye of Horus.

The post said: *"2023: Corona ended. 2026: Hantavirus."*

That was the entire post. The account, calling itself "soothsayer" with the handle @iamasoothsayer, would go on to make three more posts in the seven days that followed — short aphorisms, the kind of generic emotional epigram one would expect from a teenage Tumblr blog. *"Everyone is happy today and weep tomorrow."* *"Love is jealous with limitations."* And then, on June 17, 2022, the account fell silent.

It has not posted since.

Four years later — May 2, 2026 — the World Health Organization issued its 599th Disease Outbreak News bulletin of the post-Ebola era. The bulletin reported a multi-country cluster of hantavirus infections traced to a single cruise ship, the MV Hondius, which had departed Ushuaia, Argentina on March 20 and was now anchored off Cabo Verde with five confirmed cases, three dead, and 146 passengers from 23 countries waiting to be told whether they would be allowed to disembark. The strain was Andes hantavirus — rare, rodent-borne, and, atypically for hantaviruses, capable of human-to-human transmission. The case fatality rate hovered around forty percent.

The day the WHO bulletin was published, somebody — we believe it was a man named Jordan Crowder, posting on the platform now called X — surfaced the @iamasoothsayer post for the first time in four years. Within seventy-two hours it had been quote-tweeted by a Super Smash Bros. Melee professional with a million followers, by an aggregator account with a hundred thousand more, by Polymarket, by a Spanish constitutional law account, by Marjorie Taylor Greene, and by Libs of TikTok. The retweet count climbed past 60,000. The replies surpassed 268,000. Grok, the AI assistant native to the platform, confirmed in-thread that the post had not been edited since June 2022. We have, for the moment, no reason to dispute this.

This file is not about who @iamasoothsayer is.

We do not know. We have not tried to find out. The account has the digital signature of a Filipino aphorism account from the late Twitter-bot era; the cadence of the non-prediction posts ("Love is jealous with limitations") fits that pattern almost exactly. It is entirely possible that the account belongs to a then-teenager from Manila or Cebu who heard the word "hantavirus" in passing — perhaps from the 2020 Chinese state-media tweet that briefly trended after a single fatal hantavirus case was reported in Yunnan — and threw it into a list of bad-vibe future events the way one might throw a dart.

It is also possible that he did not.

We have spent the last seventy-two hours trying to determine, in the most general possible terms, **how alone the soothsayer is**. That is the only question this file claims to answer. We have searched, in English, in Russian, in Mandarin, in Korean, in Japanese, in Vietnamese, in Thai, and in Hindi. We have read Substacks, prediction markets, astrology blogs, conspiracy boards, esoteric forums, archive.org snapshots, Wayback Machine timestamps, fact-checks, and folk-prophecy press. We have looked at named astrologers, anonymous chatroom predictions, regulatory filings, and the published archives of every major Anglo-American epidemiology newsletter we know of. The findings are, in our view, more interesting than the soothsayer himself.

#### The wall

There are, on the public record, **nine independent voices** that put a marker on 2026 as the year of plague before 2026 began. We did not curate this list ideologically. We did not pick the famous ones and leave the rest. Each entry below is verifiable in indexed media. Each has a date that pre-dates May 2026. Each names either the year, the disease, or both.

**Pavel Globa.** May 7, 2020. Russia's most-cited living astrologer, in an interview on Radio Komsomolskaya Pravda, said: *"This is not the current coronavirus, where 2% die. If we don't prepare seriously, it will be comparable to the Spanish flu epidemic of 1918–1919."* In a separate publication on predskazanie.ru, Globa told his readers that 2026 would be a year of "sea conflicts" and warned that "straits and channels will gain much greater significance." The MV Hondius is a ship.

**Mehdi Ebragimi Vafa.** October 18, 2022. Iranian-Russian medium, winner of Russia's *Battle of Psychics* television show, in an interview with the celebrity outlet StarHit: *"A new virus will appear, more powerful than what we call COVID-19. The infection will strike nerve cells, people will go mad."* Mehdi has, like all media psychics, also predicted things that did not happen. His track record is not the point. His record is.

**Tamara Globa.** December 23, 2024. Russian astrologer, author of multiple bestselling almanacs, in StarHit: *"2025 and 2026 are pivotal years. The whole world will be reshaped."* In a separate January 2026 interview with Ura.News and Cxid.info, she went further: *"May 2026 will overturn everything."* The Hondius cluster was reported on the second of May.

**Baba Vanga.** Bulgarian seer, died 1996. Her catalog of predictions is recorded by her biographer Krasimira Stoyanova and circulates almost exclusively through Russian-language press. The most-quoted relevant lines: *"Frozen virus particles trapped in glaciers will be released by climate change and cause a new pandemic"* and, for 2026 specifically, *"One part of the world will freeze, another will burn."* Andes hantavirus is rodent-borne and originates in South America. We do not insist on the connection. We record that the connection is being insisted upon.

**Zeng Shiqiang (曾仕強).** Taiwanese I Ching scholar, died 2018. Lectures still circulate widely on the Chinese internet. The line that recurs in every transcription: *"未来5年是人类大量死亡的阶段。上天的第一个工具叫做瘟疫。这种瘟疫传播很快，无药可救。"* — *"The next five years are a phase of mass human death. Heaven's first tool is plague. It spreads fast. There is no cure."* Zeng also framed 2026 as a 丙午 year — the Red Horse year — within his cosmological reading of the 离火九运 (Li-Fire Ninth Cycle) that began in 2024.

**The 赊刀人.** A Chinese folk-prophecy tradition associated with itinerant knife sellers who, by reputation, leave behind verses that name the conditions of their return. The verse currently in circulation, published in Epoch Times on February 3, 2026 and reproduced by half a dozen Chinese outlets since: *"二六红马血火喷 / 西洋暗无天日 / 南洋米贵如珍珠 / 东洋遍地是枯骨"* — *"2026 Red Horse year, blood and fire spew. The Western Ocean is darkened sunless. In the South Seas, rice is precious as pearls. The Eastern lands are strewn with bleached bones."*

**The Tui Bei Tu (推背圖).** A 7th-century Chinese imperial prophecy book, attributed to Li Chunfeng and Yuan Tiangang. Image XLI of LX, currently being read against 2026 by both serious sinologists and amateur YouTube commentators: *"天地晦盲 草木蕃殖 / 陰陽反背 上土下日"* — *"Heaven and earth go dark. Plants multiply unchecked. Yin and Yang reverse. Earth above, sun below."* Thirteen centuries of margin to read into.

**The Year of the Red Fire Horse (丙午).** A folk-astrology trope, more cosmological than personal, that 2026 is a 丙午 year — the Red Fire Horse, considered traditionally inauspicious in Chinese, Japanese, and Korean almanacs. Major Russian outlets — Lenta, RIA, Komsomolskaya Pravda — pre-published 2026 framings in late 2025 noting that the year would open with a solar eclipse on February 17 and that, in folk reading, "solar eclipses traditionally activate viruses (cf. coronavirus 2019)." The articles ran four to six months before the Hondius docked.

**@iamasoothsayer.** June 11, 2022. The only one who named the disease.

That is the wall. Nine entries. Three continents. Six centuries of tradition collapsed into one calendar year.

#### The absence

The far stranger finding is the negative space. We searched for any Substack post, prediction market contract, mainstream Anglo-American astrologer's annual forecast, journalist's column, or epidemiologist's longform that named hantavirus and 2026 by name before May 2026. We found nothing. The earliest Polymarket contract on a 2026 hantavirus pandemic was opened on May 4, 2026 — two days after the WHO bulletin. The earliest Substack post by a credentialed epidemiologist (Jeremy Faust, "Inside Medicine") landed the same day. Eric Topol, Katelyn Jetelina, Kristen Panthagani, Bret Weinstein, Marty Makary, Vinay Prasad, Igor Chudov, Brownstone Institute — none had posted on hantavirus risk before May 2026. Chani Nicholas, Susan Miller, Jessica Adams — none had named the disease.

In English, on the indexable internet, **the @iamasoothsayer post is the only public, dated, pre-outbreak hantavirus + 2026 marker we were able to find**.

That is the entire case.

#### What this file is, and isn't

We do not assert causation. We do not allege a leak. We do not credit any individual with foreknowledge. We do not name a perpetrator, because there is no perpetrator we can responsibly name and most attempts to do so — including the recurring viral claim that Bill Gates "predicted hantavirus" in 2021 — collapse on contact with a fact-checker.

What we are doing is recording. We are recording, in public, with sources, with stamps, that nine independent voices on three continents named 2026 as a year of plague before 2026 began, and that one of those voices, an account that has spoken only four times in its life, named the disease.

The simplest explanation for any individual entry on the wall is coincidence, survivorship bias, or post-hoc reading-in. The simplest explanation for the *aggregate* — nine entries across English, Russian, Bulgarian, Mandarin, Iranian-Russian, and Tang-dynasty Chinese folk traditions — is also coincidence, survivorship bias, or post-hoc reading-in. We acknowledge this. The interesting thing is not that we have proven a conspiracy. We have not. The interesting thing is that the simplest explanation requires you to believe in a fairly large coincidence, and that the next-simplest explanation requires you to believe something stranger about how the year 2026 came to be marked, in advance, by people who had no apparent way of communicating with one another.

You may believe whichever you like. The file is open. The wall is on the website. The sources are linked. The fifth tablet is on Ethereum.
